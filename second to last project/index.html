Impact

Grace Hopper, a trailblazing computer scientist and U.S. Navy rear admiral, revolutionized the world of computing. She developed the first compiler, a groundbreaking tool that transformed how programmers interact with computers, and played a pivotal role in the creation of COBOL, a language that has stood the test of time. Hooper was not just a techincal genius; she was a visionary who championed the idea that programming languages should be accessible and machine-independent. Her legacy continues to inspire generations of technologists, making her an enduring icon in the history of computing. Explore her remarkable journey and contributions that have shaped the digital age.

Early Life and Education

Grace Brewster Hopper was born on December 9, 1906, in New York City. She displayed curiosity and passion for learning from an early age. Hopper earned a bachelor’s degree in mathematics and physics from Vassar College in 1928 and went on to complete a master’s degree and a Ph.D. in mathematics at Yale University by 1934. Before entering the Navy, she taught mathematics at Vassar College.

Career

In 1943, as World War II unfolded, Hopper left her academic post to join the U.S. Navy Reserve. Initially turned away due to age and physical standards, she succeeded in joining the WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) and was assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard, where she became one of the first programmers on the Harvard Mark I, an early electromechanical computer. After the war, Hopper joined the Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation, where she helped develop the UNIVAC I, one of the first commercial computers in the United States. At Eckert–Mauchly she also began work on what would become the world’s first compiler a program that translates human-readable instructions into machine code. Hopper strongly believed that computers should be programmed in languages close to English. Her work on Flow-Matic, one of the first English like data processing languages, helped inspire the creation of COBOL (Common Business-Oriented Language) in 1959, a high-level language designed for business use and still in operation in many legacy systems today.

Retirement

Although Hopper first retired from the Navy Reserve in 1966, she was quickly recalled to active duty to assist in standardizing computer languages across the Navy. She continued to serve, ultimately retiring in 1986 at age 79 as a rear admiral, making her one of the oldest active duty officers in the U.S. military. After her final retirement, she continued to work as a consultant and speaker in the computing industry until her death on January 1, 1992.

News

In 1982, Captain Grace Hopper delivered a lecture titled "Future Possibilities: Data, Hardware, Software, and People" at the NSA, where she discussed the evolving landscape of computing. She emphasized the rapid growth of data and the need for advancements in hardware and software to keep pace. Hopper also highlighted the critical role of people especially the next generation of computer scientists in shaping the future of techonlogy. Her insights underscored her visionary approach to computing and her belief in the limitless potential of innovation.
